## 01. Context

Agronomic specialists do not decide from an isolated number. They compare crops, regions, seasons, technologies, test history, and environmental conditions.

The system needed to support that kind of technical reasoning: slow, comparative, documented, and context-dependent.

## 02. Problem

The data existed, but analysis did not happen inside a single structure. Before interpreting a scenario, specialists had to gather scattered material and manually rebuild the basis for decision-making.

This created variation between analyses, weakened traceability, and separated the technical opinion from the context that justified it.

## 03. Previous system or operational scenario

Reports, spreadsheets, forms, and internal systems recorded pieces of the process. Each tool answered one part of the operation, but none supported the full analysis and validation cycle.

The previous scenario required the specialist to become the system integrator. The product needed to assume part of that work.

## 04. My role

I worked on information structure and analytical interfaces for technical decision support.

- Modeled crop as the central analysis unit.
- Organized layers of technical, environmental, and market indicators.
- Structured comparisons across regions, periods, and technologies.
- Connected technical analysis, validation, and institutional documentation.
- Validated the logic with field specialists and technical teams.

## 05. Constraints

The domain required depth. Oversimplifying the data could erase relevant variations between crops, regions, and seasons.

The sources also had different structures and granularities. The design had to reduce noise without creating a false sense of precision.

## 06. Design decision

The core decision was to organize the system around crop, not around the source systems.

This brought the interface closer to how specialists think. They do not start by asking which database stores the information; they start by asking what happens to a crop under a specific condition.

## 07. Solution architecture

The platform was structured as a continuous flow:

- crop view;
- technical and environmental indicators;
- comparative analysis;
- validation and technical opinion;
- institutional documentation.

Each layer needed to keep its relationship with the previous ones. Comparison only matters when users understand data origin, period, region, and condition.

## 08. Main flow

The specialist selects a crop, reviews indicators, compares scenarios, identifies relevant variations, and records the technical decision in the same environment.

The flow avoids ending the analysis in a separate document. The opinion is created inside the technical context that sustains it.

## 09. Visual evidence

The main visual evidence is the structure of comparison: agricultural data stops appearing as loose tables and begins to relate through crop, territory, period, and technology.

The design favors layered reading. First the user understands the scenario. Then compares. Only after that records a decision.

## 10. Impact

The published impact is qualitative because there is no quantitative metric with enough evidence for this case.

The structural gain was reducing fragmentation between analysis and decision: specialists could read the technical process more continuously and with stronger institutional traceability.

## 11. Limitations

Some historical sources remained in legacy formats. Data coverage also varied by region, season, and crop.

Those limits needed to appear as part of the reading. In a technical system, hiding coverage gaps is worse than exposing them clearly.

## 12. Learning

Agricultural data only becomes intelligence when it has a model. In this project, the value of design was not making indicators more attractive, but structuring comparison, context, and decision.
