## 01. Context

Business financial products carry a permanent tension: they need to feel simple to the person opening the account and rigorous to the teams responsible for risk, security, and compliance.

For small companies, that tension usually appears as bureaucracy, waiting, and lack of clarity. The product needed to reduce opacity without weakening mandatory controls.

## 02. Problem

Business users needed to understand where they were in the journey, which documents had been accepted, which validations were pending, and which services were already available.

Without that clarity, the regulated journey felt arbitrary. Users relied on support to discover what the product itself should explain.

## 03. Previous system or operational scenario

Parts of validation happened outside the main experience. Documents, internal reviews, manual approvals, and profile-based rules had little visibility for users.

The flow created passive waiting: customers submitted information and lost the reading of what happened next.

## 04. My role

I worked on structuring the regulated journey and state-based interfaces.

- Mapped steps, pending items, approvals, and transitions.
- Structured progress feedback for document validations.
- Organized financial services into activatable modules.
- Modeled variations for MEI, small companies, LTDA structures, and business profiles.
- Designed authentication, document correction, permissions, and activation flows.

## 05. Constraints

Compliance was non-negotiable. No simplification could compromise security, document validation, authentication, or traceability.

There were also real variations by company type. A MEI does not need the same permission density as a more complex corporate structure.

## 06. Design decision

The main decision was to turn regulation into product state.

Instead of hiding rules behind one long form, the system communicated steps, pending items, approvals, corrections, and available modules according to the account situation.

## 07. Solution architecture

The architecture was organized in layers:

- company opening and identification;
- document validation and authentication;
- base account;
- permissions and users;
- modular financial services;
- pending actions and follow-up.

This structure allows the experience to start simple and grow in complexity as the business profile requires.

## 08. Main flow

The user starts account opening, provides essential data, submits documentation, follows the review, corrects pending items when needed, and accesses services as they are released.

The focus was not promising speed without evidence. It was making every state understandable: what is complete, what is under review, and what depends on action.

## 09. Visual evidence

The visual evidence is in states and transitions: authentication, document submission, correction, approval, blocking, and service activation.

The design needed to make clear that the journey progressed through regulatory criteria, not an arbitrary sequence of screens.

## 10. Impact

The published impact is qualitative. Account opening and approval time metrics were not used because there was not enough documentary support for publication.

The structural gain was reducing opacity in a regulated journey, connecting documentation, risk, permissions, and service activation into a more predictable experience.

## 11. Limitations

Internal systems, manual review, and risk policies still influenced timing and decisions. The product did not control everything.

The design needed to separate what depended on the user, what was under review, and what depended on internal processes.

## 12. Learning

Regulation is not the enemy of experience. The problem begins when mandatory rules become interface silence. Making states explicit reduces anxiety and lowers dependence on support.
