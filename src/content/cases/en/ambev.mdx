## 01. Context

A national logistics operation does not break at one point. It loses synchronization through distributed signals: availability, rupture, loading, region, route, product, and commercial priority.

The challenge was to create a shared reading for teams that see the same operation through different cuts.

## 02. Problem

When a rupture appears late, the operation is already reacting with delay. The team needs to know where the problem is, which region was affected, which products matter most, and what needs priority.

Before the system, that reading depended on manual cross-checking between reports and regional views.

## 03. Previous system or operational scenario

Logistics reports, regional spreadsheets, isolated dashboards, and internal systems showed parts of the operation. The problem was not missing indicators; it was the lack of integrated operational reading.

Each area could have a different version of the same state. That made prioritization and alignment harder.

## 04. My role

I worked on structuring operational visibility and interfaces for monitoring and prioritization.

- Modeled reading by operational state.
- Separated continuous monitoring from cause investigation.
- Defined hierarchy for ruptures, alerts, loading, and availability.
- Structured navigation across national, regional, and detail levels.
- Validated flows with operation and supply chain teams.

## 05. Constraints

The system had to support different operational maturity levels, data latencies, and team needs.

Not every data point arrived with the same frequency. The design had to communicate delay, priority, and confidence without promising precision that did not exist.

## 06. Design decision

The main decision was to organize the interface by operational state, not by administrative hierarchy or source systems.

This places users in front of the right question: what needs attention now?

## 07. Solution architecture

The solution was structured in three layers:

- national distribution monitoring;
- critical indicators for availability, rupture, loading, and service level;
- contextual analysis of operational events.

This architecture allows users to move from overview to detail without breaking the reasoning thread.

## 08. Main flow

The user starts from the national reading, identifies regions that need attention, opens a rupture or deviation, and understands the context needed to prioritize action.

The flow does not try to explain everything at once. It guides from anomaly to diagnosis while preserving scale and context.

## 09. Visual evidence

The visual evidence is in the hierarchy of signals: rupture and priority need to appear before secondary metrics.

The design uses contrast between normality, attention, and deviation to turn logistics data into a shared operational reading.

## 10. Impact

The published impact is qualitative. There is no quantitative metric with enough evidence to communicate time reduction or monitored volume.

The structural gain was making the operational state more visible and shared, reducing dependence on manual reconstruction before decision-making.

## 11. Limitations

Some centers, routes, and indicators depended on batch processing. Legacy systems also imposed differences in granularity.

The product could not pretend real-time operation where there was delay. The interface needed to explain data freshness, confidence level, and the path for deeper analysis.

## 12. Learning

Monitoring is not just displaying indicators. In distributed operations, design builds a shared reading so different teams can act on the same reality.
